Executive Summary
The Software Project Management Plan for ObservAI provides a comprehensive roadmap for managing, estimating, scheduling, monitoring, and controlling the development of an AI-powered real-time customer analytics platform for cafés and restaurants. It adopts the Incremental Development Model and structures the project into three major increments aligned with academic milestones, supported by a detailed Work Breakdown Structure with clear dependencies, and milestone-driven progression. Resource planning, expected expenditures, Net Present Value analysis confirm the project’s feasibility. The total estimated direct expenditure is 13,500 TL and 140 USD, with costs minimized through reliance on open-source software, personal hardware, and lightweight AI models. Effort estimation is conducted through WBS-based estimation, Use Case Points, and Agile Story Points, with calculations presented transparently to ensure traceability and alignment with the project scope. The SPMP also outlines a rigorous monitoring and measurement framework, including weekly team meetings, structured two-week sprints, GitHub-based task tracking, mandatory peer-reviewed pull requests, and bi-weekly progress reports. Progress is evaluated through project metrics such as schedule variance and velocity, product metrics including AI accuracy, system latency, and visualization performance, and process metrics such as bug density, issue resolution rate, and test coverage. These metrics are systematically collected through GitHub Insights, Notion dashboards, and internal reports to support data-driven decisions and early detection of delays or quality risks. The plan includes a structured catalog of the complete software development environment covering programming languages, frameworks, AI libraries, databases, deployment tools, testing environments, and communication platforms to ensure consistency across all development stages. Reflections on the preparation process highlight challenges such as coordinating team schedules, interpreting estimation methodologies, and adapting AI-centric system characteristics to classical project management structures. Ethical and legal considerations, especially GDPR/KVKK compliance due to video analytics, were addressed in defining system constraints and planning workflows. Overall, the SPMP establishes a coherent and realistic strategy for executing ObservAI, ensuring orderly progress and alignment with academic objectives while supporting predictable, development across all increments.

Project Schedule
This section presents the overall project schedule of ObservAI by defining the work packages, their dependency relationships, required resources, and the allocation of budget and responsibilities among team members. 1.2. Resource Requirements
The ObservAI project is developed primarily using the existing personal computing resources of the team members. Each student uses their own personal laptop to conduct various development and testing activities. During the early stages, experiments and AI model evaluations are done locally; however, the final system would be remotely accessible by café or restaurant managers using a standard web browser.
No cloud-based GPU services will be used due to high operational costs; however, limited cloud hosting may be used for short-term backend testing purposes, as reflected in Table 2. All AI computations will be done on local machines, using lightweight models of computer vision.
Resource utilization follows the incremental schedule of the project. During the first semester, resources are primarily used for planning, requirement elicitation, and initial UI and backend development. In the second semester, computational and testing resources become more prominent as AI integration, analytics, and system-wide testing activities intensify.

Discussion
The three estimation approaches yield the following total effort ranges:
WBS Decomposition-Based Estimation:
≈ 1,530 person-hours
Use Case Point (UCP) Estimation:
≈ 1,488 person-hours
Agile Estimation (Story Points):
≈ 1,460–1,500 person-hours
Although the methods are conceptually different, all three converge to a very similar effort range (≈ 1,450–1,550 person-hours). The small variations can be explained as follows:
The WBS estimate is slightly higher because it explicitly includes management tasks, documentation (Initial Plan, SRS, SPMP), and integration/testing overhead.
The UCP estimate focuses on functional effort based on actors and use cases, and is sensitive to the assignment of technical and environmental factors.
The Agile estimate is driven by user stories and team velocity, and is more flexible; actual values will be refined as sprints are completed and empirical velocity is observed.
Given that all three estimation techniques converge within a narrow range, the Agile estimate is particularly useful for day-to-day planning because it can be continuously refined based on real sprint performance data. However, for establishing a stable and realistic baseline, the WBS estimate is the most appropriate for our project. This is because the WBS model directly reflects CTIS academic timelines, defined Work Packages, and the deliverable-oriented workload structure of ObservAI. In contrast, the UCP and Agile results serve as consistency checks that validate the WBS outcome, as both methods fall naturally within the expected variation band and confirm that the overall effort aligns with the functional size and complexity of the system.
3. Project Monitoring and Measuring
This section describes how the ObservAI project will be monitored throughout its lifecycle to ensure that development progresses according to schedule, and to identify whether the team is moving too fast or too slow relative to planned milestones. Progress will be evaluated through structured meetings, sprint-based tracking, task management tools, and well-defined quantitative metrics. These mechanisms collectively allow the team to maintain transparency, detect deviations early, and ensure that the system’s development aligns with the academic timeline and deliverable expectations.
3.1 Monitoring Progress
3.1.1 Weekly Team Meetings
Frequency: Once per week
Duration: Approximately 1 hour
Purpose:
Review completed and ongoing tasks
Identify obstacles in the AI pipeline, backend services, or dashboard integration
Coordinate upcoming work and adjust priorities
Ensure all members are synchronized regarding sprint goals
These meetings provide continuous alignment and reduce the risk of unnoticed delays.
3.1.2 Sprint Planning and Sprint Review
Sprint Duration: 2 weeks
Sprint Planning:
Held at the beginning of each sprint (approx. 2 hours)
Define sprint goals and assign tasks based on functional requirements
Prioritize critical work such as camera → AI engine integration, dashboard rendering, and zone labeling
Sprint Review:
Conducted at the end of each sprint (approx. 1 hour)
Demonstrate completed features
Gather internal and advisor feedback
Evaluate compliance with planned objectives
This rhythm ensures that the project remains predictable and adaptable.
3.1.3 Task Tracking
Task management is performed through GitHub Projects and a supplementary internal Notion board.
Tasks move through a defined workflow:
 To-Do → In Progress → Review → Done
Every task has a designated owner and a clear deadline
Requirements are decomposed into incremental deliverables to match sprint timelines
This structure ensures visibility of responsibilities and prevents bottlenecks.
3.1.4 Version Control and Code Review
Platform: GitHub
Branching Strategy: Feature-based branching
Pull Request Workflow:
All changes require at least one peer approval
Code reviews focus on maintainability, architectural consistency, and correctness
Special attention is given to AI inference modules, backend API endpoints, and dashboard visualization logic
Regular reviews maintain quality standards and prevent regression issues.
3.1.5 Progress Reports
Frequency: Every two weeks
Content:
Summary of completed work
Identified challenges
Updated estimates and next sprint objectives
Audience: Team members and academic advisor
These reports ensure transparency and make progress easy to evaluate over time.
3.2 Metrics Collection
ObservAI uses three categories of metrics—project, product, and process metrics. These metrics guide decision-making, highlight risks, and validate whether development remains healthy and aligned with expectations.
3.2.1 Project Metrics
Schedule Variance (SV)
Definition: The difference between planned and completed tasks during a sprint
Formula:
 SV = Planned Tasks – Completed Tasks
Purpose: Detect delays early and re-allocate resources if necessary
Frequency: Evaluated weekly and at each sprint review
Any non-zero variance triggers corrective action.
Team Velocity
Team velocity is estimated from early increments and refined throughout development.
Average velocity: ~6 story points per sprint
Approximate effort per story point: 35–40 hours
Used to forecast milestone completion dates, especially the first increment demo
Velocity trends help determine if the team is ahead of or behind schedule.
3.2.2 Product Metrics
AI Model Accuracy
Definition: Accuracy of person detection and demographic estimation (age/gender)
Method: Precision/recall calculation using test video datasets
Target: ≥ 80% detection accuracy
Purpose: Ensure reliability of analytics displayed on the dashboard
System Latency (End-to-End Pipeline Delay)
Definition: Time from camera input → AI inference → backend processing → dashboard update
Target: ≤ 1.5 seconds
Measurement Tools: Backend logs, Chrome DevTools performance tracking
Heatmap Rendering Performance
Definition: Smoothness and responsiveness of heatmap visualization
Target: ~30 FPS for fluid user experience
These metrics ensure that ObservAI’s real-time capabilities meet functional expectations.
3.2.3 Process Metrics
Bug Density
Definition: Number of bugs found per sprint relative to newly added code
Purpose: Identify modules with higher defect rates and prioritize refactoring
Target: Reduce critical bugs to zero by sprint end
GitHub Activity Metrics
Commit frequency
Pull request review time
Issue resolution rate
Test Coverage
Focus on backend APIs and AI inference service
Target: ≥ 70% overall (≥ 40% minimum for the first increment)
Helps ensure sufficient testing depth and reduces regression failures.
These indicators reflect team engagement and workflow stability.
3.2.4 How, When, and Where Metrics Are Stored
Storage:
GitHub Insights (commits, PRs, issues)
Notion Metric Dashboard (velocity, SV, bug counts, accuracy scores)
Internal progress report documents
Collection Frequency:
Project metrics → weekly and per sprint
Product metrics → after each major feature implementation
Process metrics → continuously via GitHub automation
3.2.5 Purpose of Collecting Metrics
Metrics are collected to:
Monitor project health and detect deviations from schedule early.
Improve team productivity through data-driven sprint planning.
Maintain product quality, especially in real-time AI components.
Support decision-making for prioritization and resource allocation.
Provide transparency to the advisor and stakeholders through quantifiable indicators.
These metrics collectively ensure that ObservAI progresses predictably and meets academic and technical expectations.

5. Discussions
This section summarizes the challenges, difficulties, and reflections experienced by the ObservAI team during the preparation of the Software Project Management Plan (SPMP). While the document served as an essential guideline for structuring project execution, its preparation presented several organizational, technical, and time-related constraints that influenced the overall process. The following subsections outline these challenges and provide insight into how the team adapted and improved throughout the development of this deliverable.
5.1 Limitations and Constraints
Time Constraints
The SPMP preparation coincided with academic deadlines, quizzes, and ongoing work for the Initial Plan and SRS. Balancing these tasks with the rigorous requirements of the management plan created significant time pressure, particularly when compiling detailed estimation tables and cross-referencing data between deliverables. Coordinating the development of multiple sections under tight deadlines required additional effort and resulted in occasional delays in task completion.
Complexity of Estimation Techniques
The SPMP required the application of three distinct estimation methods (WBS, UCP, Agile). Although these methodologies provided valuable insight, their theoretical complexity and extensive calculation steps posed a challenge. Converting ObservAI’s functionality—especially AI-related components—into quantifiable estimation units required the team to invest extra time in understanding formulas, analyzing use case complexity, and establishing realistic conversion criteria.
5.2 Team Coordination and Communication Challenges
Scheduling synchronous meetings was occasionally difficult due to conflicting course loads and examination periods. As a result, some discussions had to be postponed or conducted asynchronously through messaging platforms. Additionally, aligning the writing style and structure across different SPMP subsections required iterative revisions to ensure consistency. Despite these challenges, the team adapted by increasing communication frequency and distributing responsibilities more clearly during the later stages.
5.3 Requirement Interpretation and Document Structuring
Ensuring that the SPMP fully complied with the instructor’s expectations and template guidelines required careful interpretation of the deliverable instructions. Certain parts—such as cost modeling, NPV analysis, and resource allocation—demanded detailed clarification from external references and example documents. Designing the project monitoring and measuring framework for a real-time AI-based system (involving camera streams, inference latency, and dashboard responsiveness) required the team to translate technical metrics into project management terminology, which was initially challenging.
5.4 Technical Expertise Constraints
Some team members had limited prior experience with project management concepts such as scheduling dependencies, effort estimation, and metrics-driven monitoring. This made the early phases of preparation slower as the team spent additional time learning best practices, identifying appropriate metrics, and modeling realistic workflows. Similarly, integrating ObservAI’s AI-centric characteristics into classical SPMP structures required careful adaptation to ensure accuracy without over-technicalizing the document.
5.5 Reflections on the SPMP Preparation Process
Preparing the SPMP improved the team’s understanding of structured project management and the importance of planning before implementation. Several key insights were gained:
The necessity of incremental planning, especially for AI-related components that evolve through experimentation.
The value of consistent communication and shared documentation tools to maintain alignment.
The importance of metrics-based monitoring clarified how progress and quality will be evaluated throughout development.
The benefit of establishing clear role distribution, particularly when working with multi-component systems such as ObservAI (AI engine, backend services, dashboard UI).
Overall, despite the challenges faced, the SPMP preparation strengthened the team’s organizational capabilities and improved their ability to predict, manage, and justify effort allocation for a complex real-time analytics system.
5.6 Ethical and Legal Considerations
While preparing the document, the team acknowledged the importance of adhering to privacy principles due to ObservAI’s use of video-based analytics. Although SPMP preparation itself did not involve direct ethical dilemmas, the team remained aware of the significance of GDPR/KVKK-compliant data handling practices and incorporated relevant constraints into project planning.
5.7 Multidisciplinary Aspects
The preparation primarily involved software engineering, project management, and AI system design perspectives. Although external multidisciplinary contributions were not required for this deliverable, the process highlighted the relevance of combining technical and managerial competencies to produce a coherent and realistic project plan.
